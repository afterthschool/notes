# Life Processes

The maintenance functions of living organisms must go on even when they are not doing anything particular. Even when we are just sitting in class, even if we are just asleep, this maintenance job has to go on. The processes which together perform this maintenance job are called **life processes**.

Since these maintenance processes are needed to prevent damage and breakdown, energy is required. This energy comes from outside the body of the organism. Therefore, there must be a process to transfer a source of energy from outside the body (food) to the inside. This process is called **nutrition**.

If the organism is to grow in size, additional raw materials will also be needed from outside. Since life on Earth depends on carbon-based molecules, most food sources are also carbon-based. Depending on the complexity of these carbon sources, different organisms use different kinds of nutritional processes.

The outside sources of energy can be varied because the environment is not under the control of the organism. These energy sources must be broken down or built up in the body and converted into a uniform source of energy that can be used for various molecular activities needed to maintain life and growth.

For this, a series of chemical reactions are necessary. **Oxidation-reduction reactions** are commonly used to break down molecules. Many organisms use oxygen from the environment for this purpose. The process of acquiring oxygen and using it to break down food for cellular needs is called **respiration**.

In single-celled organisms, specific organs for food intake, gas exchange, or waste removal are not needed because the entire surface is in contact with the environment. However, as body size increases and organisms become more complex, not all cells are in direct contact with the environment. Therefore, simple diffusion is not sufficient.

In multicellular organisms, different body parts are specialized to perform specific functions. These specialized tissues handle tasks such as food intake and oxygen absorption. However, this creates a challenge because food and oxygen are taken in at one location, but all cells need them. Thus, a **transport system** is required to carry these materials throughout the body.

When chemical reactions use carbon sources and oxygen for energy production, they generate waste products that may be harmful. These wastes must be removed from the body through a process called **excretion**. Specialized tissues carry out excretion, and the transport system also helps move waste from cells to these tissues.
