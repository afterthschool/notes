# 6.1 Animals – Nervous System

In animals, control and coordination are provided by **nervous and muscular tissues**, which we have studied in Class IX.

Touching a hot object is an urgent and dangerous situation for us. We need to detect it and respond quickly.

## Detection of Stimuli

How do we detect that we are touching a hot object?

All information from our environment is detected by the **specialised tips of nerve cells**, called **receptors**. These receptors are usually located in our sense organs, such as:
- Inner ear
- Nose
- Tongue

Different types of receptors detect different stimuli:
- **Gustatory receptors** detect taste
- **Olfactory receptors** detect smell

## Transmission of Nerve Impulse

The information acquired at the dendritic tip of a neuron (nerve cell) sets off a **chemical reaction**, which creates an **electrical impulse**.

![Figure6.1](../../../assets/class-10-ap/biological-science/chapter-6/section1/figure1.png)

This impulse travels in the following path:
1. From the **dendrite**
2. To the **cell body**
3. Along the **axon** to its end

At the end of the axon:
- The electrical impulse triggers the release of **chemicals (neurotransmitters)**
- These chemicals cross the gap between neurons called the **synapse**
- They initiate a similar electrical impulse in the dendrite of the next neuron

This is the general mechanism by which **nervous impulses travel in the body**.

## Communication with Other Cells

The process continues until the impulse reaches its target, such as:
- **Muscle cells**
- **Gland cells**

At this point, the signal is transmitted via a synapse from the neuron to these cells.

## Structure and Function of Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is made up of an organised network of **nerve cells (neurons)**.

It is specialised for:
- Conducting information
- Transmitting electrical impulses
- Coordinating activities across the body

# Sensory Perception and Reflex Actions

## Taste and Smell Connection

Is there a difference in how sugar and food taste if your nose is blocked?

Yes, there is a noticeable difference. This happens because:
- **Taste and smell work together** to create the full experience of flavor.
- When the nose is blocked (like during a cold), **olfactory receptors** cannot detect smell properly.
- As a result, food may taste **bland or less flavorful**, even if the taste receptors are working fine.

A similar situation occurs when we have a **cold**, where nasal blockage reduces our ability to smell, thereby affecting taste.

---

## 6.1.1 What Happens in Reflex Actions?

The term **reflex** is commonly used to describe sudden actions in response to environmental changes.

### Examples of Reflex Actions:
- Jumping away from an oncoming bus
- Pulling your hand back from a flame
- Mouth watering when hungry

In all these cases:
- The action happens **without conscious thinking**
- We do not feel fully in control of the response

These are automatic responses to stimuli in the environment.

---

## Reflex vs Thinking

Let us consider the example of touching a flame.

One possible way to respond is:
1. Feel the pain
2. Think about the danger
3. Decide to move the hand

However:
- **Thinking takes time**
- It involves complex interactions of many nerve impulses
- This delay could result in injury (burn)

---

## Role of the Brain

The **brain**:
- Is located at the front of the skull
- Contains a dense network of neurons
- Receives signals from all parts of the body
- Processes information before sending responses

For this process:
- Nerves carry signals **to the brain**
- The brain processes them
- Signals are sent back to muscles for action

But this pathway is **too slow for emergency situations** like touching a hot object.

---

## Reflex Arc: The Fast Response System

To avoid delays, the body uses a faster mechanism called a **reflex arc**.

### What is a Reflex Arc?

A **reflex arc** is a simple neural pathway that:
- Bypasses the brain for immediate action
- Connects sensory (input) nerves directly to motor (output) nerves

### How It Works:
1. Receptors detect a stimulus (e.g., heat)
2. Sensory neurons carry the signal to the spinal cord
3. The spinal cord directly connects to motor neurons
4. Motor neurons send signals to muscles
5. Muscles respond quickly (e.g., pulling hand away)

---

## Role of the Spinal Cord

- Nerves from all over the body meet in the **spinal cord**
- Reflex arcs are formed here
- The spinal cord enables **quick responses without waiting for the brain**

However:
- The information is still sent to the brain afterward
- This allows us to become aware of the action

---

## Key Idea

Reflex actions are:
- **Automatic**
- **Fast**
- **Protective responses**

They ensure that the body reacts quickly to dangerous situations without waiting for conscious thought.

# Reflex Actions and Human Brain

## Evolution and Importance of Reflex Arcs

Reflex arcs have evolved in animals because the thinking process of the brain is not fast enough for immediate responses.

- Many animals have little or no complex neural networks required for thinking.
- Reflex arcs provide an **efficient and quick way of responding** in the absence of thought processes.
- Even in organisms with complex brains, reflex arcs remain important because they enable **rapid responses to dangerous situations**.

1. Light stimulus is detected by receptors in the eyes
2. Sensory neurons carry the signal
3. Signal reaches the central nervous system
4. Motor neurons send response signals
5. Eye muscles adjust (e.g., pupil constriction)

---

## 6.1.2 Human Brain

Reflex action is not the only function of the spinal cord.

- The spinal cord carries information to the brain.
- The brain is responsible for **thinking, decision-making, and coordination**.

### Central Nervous System (CNS)

The **brain and spinal cord together form the Central Nervous System (CNS)**.

Functions of CNS:
- Receives information from all parts of the body
- Integrates and processes this information
- Sends responses accordingly

---

## Voluntary Actions

Humans are capable of **voluntary actions**, which involve conscious thinking and decision-making.

### Examples:
- Writing
- Talking
- Moving a chair
- Clapping

In such actions:
- The brain decides what to do
- Signals are sent to muscles to perform the action

---

## Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

Communication between the CNS and the rest of the body occurs through the **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**.

The PNS consists of:
- **Cranial nerves** (arising from the brain)
- **Spinal nerves** (arising from the spinal cord)

---

## Functions of the Brain

The brain:
- Allows us to think and analyze
- Integrates sensory inputs
- Controls voluntary movements
- Coordinates body activities

---

## Major Parts of the Brain

The brain is divided into three major regions:

1. **Fore-brain**
2. **Mid-brain**
3. **Hind-brain**

---

## Fore-brain (Main Thinking Part)

The **fore-brain** is the most important part for thinking and decision-making.

### Functions:
- Receives sensory impulses from receptors
- Has specialized areas for:
  - Sight
  - Hearing
  - Smell
- Contains **association areas** where:
  - Information from different senses is combined
  - Past experiences are used for interpretation

### Decision-Making Process:
1. Sensory information is received
2. Information is interpreted
3. Decision is made
4. Signal is sent to motor areas
5. Muscles perform the action

---

## Internal Sensations

Not all sensations are external.

Example:
- Feeling full after eating

This is controlled by:
- A **hunger regulation center** located in a specific part of the fore-brain

---

## Key Takeaways

- Reflex actions are fast and automatic
- The brain enables thinking and voluntary actions
- CNS and PNS work together for coordination
- The fore-brain plays a major role in decision-making and sensory processing

# Functions of Brain, Protection, and Muscle Action

## Understanding Functions of Brain Parts

Study the labelled diagram of the human brain. We know that different parts of the brain have specific functions. By analyzing the diagram, we can identify the role of each part.

---

## Reflex and Involuntary Actions

We have already discussed simple reflex actions. However, there are other types of actions that occur without conscious control.

### Examples:
- Mouth watering when we see food
- Heart beating continuously
- Breathing
- Digestion

These actions:
- Do not require conscious thinking
- Cannot be easily controlled by will

---

## Types of Actions

There are three broad types of actions:

1. **Simple Reflex Actions**
   - Immediate responses (e.g., pupil size change)

2. **Voluntary Actions**
   - Controlled by thinking (e.g., moving a chair)

3. **Involuntary Actions**
   - Not under conscious control (e.g., heartbeat, digestion)

---

## Role of Mid-brain and Hind-brain

Many involuntary actions are controlled by:
- **Mid-brain**
- **Hind-brain**

### Medulla (Part of Hind-brain)

The **medulla** controls vital involuntary functions such as:
- Blood pressure
- Salivation
- Vomiting

---

## Role of Cerebellum

The **cerebellum** (part of the hind-brain) is responsible for:

- Precision of voluntary movements
- Maintaining posture
- Maintaining body balance

### Examples:
- Walking in a straight line
- Riding a bicycle
- Picking up a pencil

Without the cerebellum:
- Movements would become uncoordinated
- Balance and posture would be affected

---

## 6.1.3 How Are These Tissues Protected?

The brain is a delicate and important organ, so it requires strong protection.

### Protection Mechanisms:

- The brain is enclosed within a **bony box (skull)**
- It is surrounded by a **fluid-filled structure**, which acts as a shock absorber

### Protection of Spinal Cord:

- The **vertebral column (backbone)** protects the spinal cord
- It is a hard, bumpy structure felt along the back

---

## 6.1.4 How Does Nervous Tissue Cause Action?

Nervous tissue:
- Collects information
- Transmits signals
- Processes information
- Makes decisions
- Sends signals to muscles

### Role of Muscles

When an action needs to be performed:
- **Muscle tissue executes the final response**

---

## How Do Muscles Move?

When a nerve impulse reaches a muscle:

1. The muscle fibre responds
2. Muscle cells change their shape
3. The cells **shorten (contract)**
4. This results in movement

---

## Mechanism of Muscle Movement

Muscle movement occurs due to:
- Special **proteins inside muscle cells**
- These proteins:
  - Change shape
  - Rearrange within the cell
  - Cause contraction (shortening)

---

## Types of Muscles

From previous knowledge (Class IX), muscles are of two main types:

### 1. Voluntary Muscles
- Under conscious control
- Example: Moving hands, walking

### 2. Involuntary Muscles
- Not under conscious control
- Example: Heartbeat, digestion

---

## Key Takeaways

- Brain parts have specialized functions
- Involuntary actions are controlled by mid-brain and hind-brain
- Cerebellum ensures balance and coordination
- Brain and spinal cord are well-protected
- Nervous tissue communicates signals to muscles
- Muscles move by contracting due to protein changes
